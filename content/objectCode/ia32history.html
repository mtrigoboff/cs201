CS201: History of the IA-32 Architecture

First a key term, Computer Architecture. Much like a building's architecture, this is the “look and feel” of a computer system. This encompasses how a computer system will interface with software and hardware, what instructions can it execute, what protocols are used to read and write data, etc.

IA-32 stands for: Intel Architecture, 32 bits, also known as the x86 architecture.

The IA-32 architecture is quite old, starting back around 1980. This long evolutionary history is quite visible in the architecture, in the same way that the evolutionary history of whales can be discerned from the presence of tiny vestigial leg bones hidden within their bodies.

You can see this clearly in some of the register naming conventions. EAX is the name of a 32 bit register. AX is the name of the low-order 16 bits of that register. When Intel chips were 16 bit machines, AX was one of the registers. Now it’s half of EAX.

When Intel chips were 8 bit machines, there was an A register. AX stands for “A eXtended.” EAX stands for “Extended AX.” So the full decoding of EAX would be “Extended A eXtended.” The evolution of Intel’s chips from 8 bits to 32 bits can be seen in these naming conventions.

Another example of this is the “segment registers.” These were originally used to support an older memory access method called “segmented addressing.” We now typically use a more modern method called “flat addressing,” and the segment registers have been repurposed to support other functions such as operating system privilege levels.

Originally there were 4 segment registers: CS (code segment), DS (data segment), SS (stack segment), and ES (extra segment). More modern IA-32 chips have 2 additional segment registers: FS and GS. Intel apparently noticed they already had registers starting with C, D, and E, so they named the next 2 starting with F and G. C, D, and E had a mnemonic meaning. F and G do not. Evolution sometimes produces strange and arbitrary results.

Intel's first general purpose microprocessor was the 8086. Intel became a well known brand in the 1990's during the time of the 80386 and 80486 processors. The 5th generation processor was named the Pentium™ Processor. The move from numbers to trademarked names was done to reduce confusion and "false advertising" by competitors, because Intel discovered that it could not trademark a number like “586”. For example, one competitor (Cyrix) manufactured a “486” processor that was closer to a "“386.”

Sixth generation: Pentium™ Pro - was not even close to a Pentium, but the name stuck

Seventh generation: Pentium™ 4 (since Pentium 2 and Pentium 3 were incremental developments based on the Pentium Pro)

Eight generation: Core 2 Duo - based off the Pentium Pro, not the Pentium 4 - this is an interesting story. The main reason behind this switch was the Pentium 4 consumed too much power in the quest for faster processors. The Core 2 Duo went "back to basics" and focused on performance improvements in other ways.

Ninth generation: Core i7 (Nehalem).
